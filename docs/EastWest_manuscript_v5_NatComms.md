# Archaic hominin genomes reveal an East-West metabolic axis underlying type 2 diabetes disparities

**E. García-Morán***

Associate Professor, Department of Cell Biology, Genetics, Histology and Pharmacology, Faculty of Medicine, Universidad de Valladolid, Spain

*Correspondence: emilio.garcia.moran@uva.es | ORCID: 0000-0002-2487-6686*

*Running title: East-West metabolic axis in hominin evolution*

---

## ABSTRACT

Ethnic disparities in type 2 diabetes (T2D) risk at equivalent BMI thresholds are well-documented but mechanistically unexplained. Here we present paleogenomic evidence for a bidirectional metabolic polarization hypothesis: T2D heterogeneity reflects two independent adaptive architectures — Eastern and Western — shaped over >65,000 years of divergent thermal and nutritional selection pressure in archaic hominin lineages. Mapping five high-coverage archaic genomes (spanning 430,000 years) to ten metabolic candidate genes, we identify 168 Eastern-specific and 67 Western-specific variants after damage filtering, projecting symmetrically onto modern population frequencies (86% polarization in both directions). A single gene — *SLC2A3* (GLUT3, neuronal glucose transporter) — shows zero Eastern-specific variants across all examined hominins (Fisher's exact p = 0.008), establishing brain glucose priority as an evolutionary invariant around which both architectures were independently organized. These findings extend the thrifty genotype hypothesis to a bidirectional East-West axis and provide a mechanistic framework for ethnicity-specific T2D risk stratification and pharmacogenomic targeting.

## INTRODUCTION

Ethnic disparities in T2D risk at equivalent BMI values have been robustly documented epidemiologically: south Asian populations reach equivalent T2D incidence at BMI 23.9 kg/m² compared with 30.0 kg/m² in White populations; Chinese and Arab populations at BMI 26.9 and 26.6 kg/m² respectively¹. Whether these disparities reflect body composition differences, lifestyle factors, or the genetic architecture of T2D "remains unclear"¹ — a gap that current precision medicine frameworks have not closed.

We propose that this gap has a deep evolutionary explanation. The metabolic heterogeneity observed across ancestral groups is the signature of two independent adaptive solutions to the same biological problem: sustaining the energetic demands of an oversized brain under contrasting thermal and nutritional environments. Our framework extends the thrifty genotype hypothesis² by proposing not one but two distinct adaptive strategies, polarized along an East-West axis determined by ancestral thermal environment.

Individuals carrying Eastern metabolic architecture may present as clinically unremarkable despite elevated adiposity — colloquially *"fat but yet fit"* — a phenotype reflecting transient metabolic compensation rather than genuine metabolic resilience. This apparent fitness represents an Arctic-optimized system operating near its evolutionary design limits in a calorically abundant environment, prone to catastrophic rather than gradual decompensation.

To test this hypothesis, we leverage the recently published high-coverage genome of Denisova 17 (D17)³ — the first Eastern Neandertal genome at sufficient coverage for metabolic gene analysis — combined with Vindija 33.19 (Western Neandertal)⁴, Ust'-Ishim (early Siberian modern human)⁵, and the Tyrolean Iceman (Neolithic Alpine farmer)⁶, whose genome carries T2D and obesity risk alleles consistent with Western metabolic architecture. The Sima de los Huesos specimens (~430 ka, n=5)⁷ provide the deepest temporal anchor.

## RESULTS

### Homo sapiens occupies a unique allometric position defined by encephalization and cold adaptation

*(Fig. 1)*

Mahalanobis distance analysis in allometric space (52 mammalian species; EQ residuals, BMR z-scores, longevity z-scores, habitat temperature) identified *Homo sapiens* as a significant multivariate outlier (D = 28.81, p = 3.4 × 10⁻⁵, FDR < 0.01). When habitat temperature was incorporated, *H. sapiens* occupied the quadrant of extreme encephalization combined with cold-adapted habitat — otherwise restricted to boreal carnivores (*Vulpes lagopus*, *Canis lupus*, *Ursus americanus*) and fossorial hibernators (*Marmota marmota*) (**Fig. 1A, B**). This convergent positioning motivates a search for divergent metabolic strategies associated with sustaining a costly brain under Arctic conditions.

### Two divergent metabolic gene signatures in the human lineage

*(Fig. 2)*

Pairwise dN/dS analysis (yn00, PAML 4.9j; human vs. 13 mammalian species; 79 candidate genes from KEGG pathways hsa00010, hsa04910, hsa04714; 890 valid species pairs) identified ten genes with mean ω > 1.0, including *PKM* (ω = 2.44), *PFKM* (ω = 1.77), *PPARGC1A* (ω = 1.24), and *TRPA1* (ω = 1.05) (**Fig. 2A**). *TRPA1* showed extreme divergence versus *Ursus americanus* (ω = 3.63) and *Vulpes vulpes* (ω = 1.45), consistent with positive selection on cold thermosensation.

In marked contrast, *SLC2A3* showed the lowest mean ω of all 79 genes examined (ω = 0.21), indicating extreme purifying selection on cerebral glucose supply (**Fig. 2A**, arrow).

### Paleogenomic evidence for two independent metabolic architectures

*(Fig. 3)*

Five archaic/ancient genomes were mapped to ten candidate gene loci (~600 kb): D17 (Eastern Neandertal, ~110 ka, 37×), Vindija 33.19 (Western Neandertal, ~49 ka, 30×), Ust'-Ishim (~65 ka, 42×), and Ötzi the Tyrolean Iceman (~5.3 ka, 15.3×). After damage filtering (C→T and G→A transitions excluded), three-way intersection analysis identified **168 Eastern-specific variants** (D17 + Ust'-Ishim, absent Vindija) and **67 Western-specific variants** (D17 + Vindija, absent Ust'-Ishim; χ² = 34.1, p < 10⁻⁸) (**Fig. 3A**).

Eastern architecture was dominated by *PFKM* (42 Eastern vs. 0 Western variants; muscle phosphofructokinase), *ADIPOQ* (23 vs. 0; adiponectin), and *TRPA1* (21 vs. 2; cold thermosensor). Functional annotation (VEP) revealed predominantly intronic (45%) and regulatory (34%) variants, with 4 missense and 2 splice-acceptor variants in *LDHA* — none detected in gnomAD v4, indicating purifying selection in modern populations (**Fig. 3B**).

Western architecture showed a two-layer structure. Vindija-specific variants were dominated by *ACACA* (20; acetyl-CoA carboxylase) and *PPARGC1A* (8). Ötzi-specific variants included *PPARGC1A* (27), *ADIPOQ* (14), and *TRPA1* (14). Vindija and Ötzi share only **11 variants**, suggesting Western T2D risk was assembled independently in Pleistocene Neandertals and Neolithic farmers rather than inherited from a common ancestor (**Fig. 3C**). This interpretation is supported by Ötzi's genome: 90% Anatolian farmer ancestry, zero Steppe ancestry, and T2D risk alleles already present at ~5.3 ka⁶.

### Both architectures project symmetrically onto modern population diversity

*(Fig. 3D–F)*

Of 14 Eastern variants present in gnomAD v4, **12/14 (86%)** showed higher frequency in AMR/EAS than NFE (binomial p = 0.006). Peak polarization: *UGP2* (AMR ~25%, EAS ~53%, NFE ~3%) and *PKM* chr15:72410596 (45-fold enrichment in Eastern-ancestry populations) (**Fig. 3E**). Of 7 Western variants, **6/7 (86%)** showed higher frequency in NFE than AMR/EAS (binomial p = 0.063; **Fig. 3F**). The symmetrical polarization provides bidirectional evidence for the East-West metabolic axis. The predominance of regulatory variants (79% Eastern, 82% Western) implies that current clinical gene panels, focused on coding variants, systematically underrepresent the regulatory architecture through which ancestral metabolic adaptations persist in modern populations.

### SLC2A3 is a conserved brain glucose invariant across 430,000 years of hominin evolution

*(Fig. 4)*

*SLC2A3* showed the lowest ω of all candidate genes (ω = 0.21) and zero Eastern-specific variants in high-coverage archaic genomes. Read coverage at *SLC2A3* was equivalent to *PPARGC1A* and *TRPA1* — both showing 21-27 variants in the same specimens — confirming the absence is genuine. A 2×2 contingency table (variant-covered positions vs. total covered positions) confirmed significance: Fisher's exact p = 0.008 vs. *PPARGC1A* and p = 0.011 vs. *TRPA1* (**Fig. 4A, C**).

Targeted pileup of *SLC2A3* in merged Sima de los Huesos specimens (n=5, ~430 ka; mean depth 1.18× over 392 positions) showed reference alleles at all 3×-covered positions (n=20). Due to the inherently low coverage of 430,000-year-old specimens, these data are suggestive rather than definitive; however, the contrast with variant-rich genes in the same BAM is informative (**Fig. 4B**).

*"The brain demanded its glucose whether the hominin was hunting in the Sierra de Atapuerca, freezing in the Altai Mountains, or farming in the Danube Valley. That invariant — written in SLC2A3 — is the silent partner in the East-West diabetes story: while the periphery adapted, the brain never compromised."*

## DISCUSSION

### Two solutions to the same problem

Our data support a model in which two distinct metabolic strategies evolved independently to sustain an energetically expensive brain under contrasting thermal pressures — extending the thrifty genotype hypothesis² to a bidirectional East-West axis.

The **Eastern architecture** — enhanced glycolysis (*PFKM*, *LDHA*), augmented adipose storage (*ADIPOQ*), heightened cold thermosensation (*TRPA1*) — was fixed over >30,000 years of Arctic selection, traceable to Eastern Neandertal lineage (~110 ka) and early Siberian modern humans (~65 ka). The peopling of the Americas (~15-20 ka) exported this architecture to tropical environments, initiating an evolutionary mismatch intensified by post-contact dietary patterns. The Eastern "fat but yet fit" phenotype represents a system near its compensatory limit — prone to catastrophic decompensation rather than gradual deterioration, explaining the earlier age of onset and more aggressive presentation of T2D in Native American populations.

The **Western architecture** — lipogenesis (*ACACA*) and thermogenic regulation (*PPARGC1A*) assembled in two independent Pleistocene and Neolithic layers — reflects adaptation to temperate conditions and agricultural subsistence. Western T2D risk predates modernity: Ötzi already harbored risk alleles and a "slow metabolizer" phenotype optimized for plant-based diets 5,300 years ago⁶.

### Regulatory ghost adaptation and incomplete purification

*LDHA* missense variants shared between D17 and Ust'-Ishim are absent from gnomAD v4, indicating active purifying selection. Yet the surrounding regulatory landscape persists at 25–53% frequency in AMR/EAS populations. We term this **regulatory ghost adaptation**: coding changes eliminated, regulatory architecture co-selected with them persisting. Non-coding regulatory variants are purged slowly, particularly when their phenotypic cost manifests only under calorically abundant conditions historically novel in evolutionary terms. Ten thousand years since the Last Glacial Maximum and 500 years since contact with Western dietary patterns represent at most 400–500 human generations — insufficient to dismantle polygenic regulatory architectures.

### The human singularity and its metabolic price

*Homo sapiens* uniquely combined extreme encephalization with cold-environment colonization, enabled by behavioral and technological innovations — hunting, fire, shelter, social cooperation. Both Eastern and Western lineages paid a metabolic price for this combination, and those prices are paid today in diabetes clinics across the Americas, Europe, and Asia. As globalization and admixture render continental ancestry labels increasingly imprecise, the East-West axis retains clinical utility because it is defined by specific alleles — not self-reported ethnicity — that will remain informative even as individuals no longer identify with any single continental group.

### Implications for precision medicine

SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, and thiazolidinediones were validated predominantly in European-ancestry cohorts. The regulatory persistence of Eastern *PFKM* and *ADIPOQ* variants at high frequency in AMR populations suggests differential glycolytic and adipogenic drug responses irrespective of coding variant status. PPARγ agonists may show differential efficacy due to *ADIPOQ* polarization. The BMI thresholds documented by Caleyachetty et al.¹ are not arbitrary — they are the epidemiological shadow of the evolutionary divergence documented here.

### Limitations

This analysis is limited by available high-coverage archaic genomes; Chagyrskaya 8, Loschbour, and Stuttgart LBK are not included. The conservative C→T/G→A damage filter may remove genuine variants; however, PMDtools-based filtering (threshold ≥3) gave comparable directional results. The Sima de los Huesos SLC2A3 pileup is suggestive rather than definitive. Pairwise yn00 reflects lineage-wide divergence; branch-specific analyses are warranted. Denisovan contribution to Eastern variants was not assessed due to insufficient coverage.

## METHODS

### Allometric analysis

Mammalian allometric data were compiled from PanTHERIA (Jones et al. 2009), Stephan et al. (1981), Isler & van Schaik (2006), and Burger et al. (2019). Encephalization quotient (EQ) residuals were computed from log-log regression of brain mass on body mass. Basal metabolic rate (BMR) and longevity were z-score normalized. Habitat temperature was assigned from species range centroids. Mahalanobis distances were computed in R (stats package) for two models: Model 1 (EQ, BMR, longevity; n=28 species with complete data) and Model 2 (EQ, BMR, longevity, temperature; n=22 terrestrial species). Statistical significance was assessed by chi-squared distribution (df=4); FDR correction applied across all species.

### Candidate gene selection and dN/dS analysis

Seventy-nine candidate genes were selected from KEGG pathways hsa00010 (glycolysis/gluconeogenesis), hsa04910 (insulin signalling), and hsa04714 (thermogenesis). Coding sequences were retrieved from Ensembl (release 109) for *Homo sapiens* and 13 mammalian species selected for genome annotation quality. Pairwise dN/dS ratios were computed using yn00 (PAML 4.9j) for all human-species pairs with alignable CDS of ≥150 codons and no internal stop codons (890 valid pairs from 79 genes × 13 species). Mean ω per gene was computed across all species comparisons.

### Archaic genome mapping and variant calling

Raw sequencing reads were downloaded from ENA (D17: PRJEB108287; Vindija: PRJEB21157; Ust'-Ishim: PRJEB6622; Ötzi: PRJEB56570; Sima de los Huesos n=5: PRJEB10597). Reads were mapped to hg38 (chromosomes 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 11, 12, 15, 16, 17) using BWA-MEM v0.7.17 (-k17 -T30). Duplicate reads were not removed (low-coverage ancient DNA). Alignments were filtered at MAPQ ≥ 20. Variants were called with bcftools mpileup/call (-q20 -Q20, --ploidy 2, QUAL > 30) restricted to ten candidate gene loci (~600 kb; BED file available at GitHub). All C→T and G→A transitions were excluded as a conservative ancient DNA damage filter. Comparable results were obtained with PMDtools (threshold ≥3, data not shown).

### East-West intersection analysis

Three-way variant intersection was performed with bcftools isec (v1.17). Eastern-specific variants were defined as present in D17 and Ust'-Ishim but absent from Vindija (pattern 110). Western-specific variants were defined as present in D17 and Vindija but absent from Ust'-Ishim (pattern 101). Only single-nucleotide variants (REF and ALT length = 1) passing damage filter were retained.

### Functional annotation

Eastern-specific variants (n=282 before damage filtering; n=168 after) were annotated using Ensembl VEP (GRCh38, release 109) via REST API. Most severe consequence per variant was recorded.

### gnomAD population frequency analysis

Allele frequencies for Eastern- and Western-specific variants were retrieved from gnomAD v4 via GraphQL API (gnomad.broadinstitute.org). Population frequencies were computed as AC/AN for AMR, EAS, NFE, and AFR populations. Binomial tests (one-tailed, H₀: p=0.5) were used to assess directional polarization.

### SLC2A3 invariance analysis

For Fisher's exact test, a 2×2 contingency table was constructed per gene pair: (variant-positive covered positions, total covered positions) for *SLC2A3* vs. comparator gene in the same merged archaic BAM. Sima de los Huesos BAMs (n=5) were merged with samtools merge. Depth at *SLC2A3* locus was computed with samtools depth.

### Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were performed in R (v4.3.1). Mahalanobis distances: stats::mahalanobis(). Chi-squared tests: stats::chisq.test(). Binomial tests: stats::binom.test(). Fisher's exact tests: stats::fisher.test(). FDR correction: stats::p.adjust(method="BH").
