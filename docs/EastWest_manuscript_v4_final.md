# Paleogenomic Evidence for Two Independent Metabolic Architectures Underlying Ethnic Disparities in Type 2 Diabetes: An East-West Axis Traced Through 430,000 Years of Hominin Evolution

**E. García-Morán**

Associate Professor, Department of Cell Biology, Genetics, Histology and Pharmacology, Faculty of Medicine, Universidad de Valladolid, Spain

*Correspondence: emilio.garcia.moran@uva.es | ORCID: 0000-0002-2487-6686*

*Running title: East-West metabolic polarization in hominin evolution*

*Date: April 2026*

---

## ABSTRACT

Ethnicity-specific disparities in type 2 diabetes (T2D) risk at equivalent BMI thresholds are well-documented but mechanistically unexplained. Here we present paleogenomic evidence for a **bidirectional metabolic polarization hypothesis**: T2D heterogeneity across ancestral groups reflects two independent adaptive architectures — Eastern and Western — shaped over >65,000 years of divergent thermal and nutritional selection pressure, traceable to archaic hominin lineages predating anatomically modern humans. Using high-coverage archaic genomes spanning 430,000 years (Sima de los Huesos ~430 ka, Denisova 17 ~110 ka, Vindija 33.19 ~49 ka, Ust'-Ishim ~65 ka, Tyrolean Iceman ~5.3 ka), we identify 168 Eastern-specific and 67 Western-specific variants in ten metabolic candidate genes after conservative filtering for ancient DNA damage signatures, projecting symmetrically onto modern AMR/EAS versus NFE population frequencies (86% polarization rate in both directions; Eastern p = 0.006, Western p = 0.063 trending toward significance, limited by small gnomAD representation). A single gene — *SLC2A3*, encoding the high-affinity neuronal glucose transporter GLUT3 — shows zero Eastern-specific variants across 430,000 years of hominin evolution, establishing brain glucose priority as the non-negotiable evolutionary constant around which both metabolic architectures were independently organized (Fisher's exact p = 0.008 vs. other candidate genes). These findings provide a mechanistic framework — extending the thrifty genotype hypothesis (Neel 1962) to a bidirectional East-West axis — for ethnicity-specific T2D risk stratification and pharmacogenomic targeting.

## INTRODUCTION

Ethnicity-specific differences in T2D risk at equivalent BMI values have been robustly documented in large epidemiological cohorts. South Asian populations reach equivalent T2D incidence at BMI 23.9 kg/m² compared with 30.0 kg/m² in White populations; Chinese and Arab populations show equivalent risk at BMI 26.9 and 26.6 kg/m² respectively (Caleyachetty et al., *Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol*, 2021, n=1,472,819). Whether these disparities reflect differences in body composition, biochemical characteristics, lifestyle factors, or the genetic architecture of T2D "remains unclear" (ibid.) — a gap that current precision medicine frameworks have not closed.

We propose that this gap has a deep evolutionary explanation. The metabolic heterogeneity observed across ancestral groups is not random variation but the signature of two independent adaptive solutions to the same biological problem: sustaining the energetic demands of an oversized brain under contrasting thermal and nutritional environments. Our framework extends the thrifty genotype hypothesis (Neel 1962) by proposing not one but two distinct adaptive strategies, polarized along an East-West axis determined by ancestral thermal environment.

Individuals carrying Eastern metabolic architecture may present as clinically unremarkable despite elevated adiposity — colloquially *"fat but yet fit"* — a phenotype we propose reflects transient metabolic compensation rather than genuine metabolic resilience. This apparent fitness is the signature of an Arctic-optimized system operating near its evolutionary design limits in a calorically abundant environment. The "metabolically healthy obese" phenotype, in this framework, is not a stable state but a transient compensation that may collapse catastrophically rather than deteriorating gradually, particularly in individuals carrying Eastern metabolic architecture.

To test this hypothesis, we leverage the recently published high-coverage genome of Denisova 17 (D17; Massilani et al. 2026, *PNAS*) — the first Eastern Neandertal genome at sufficient coverage for metabolic gene analysis — combined with Vindija 33.19 (Western Neandertal; Prüfer et al. 2017), Ust'-Ishim (early Siberian modern human; Fu et al. 2014), and the Tyrolean Iceman (Neolithic Alpine farmer; Wang et al. 2023, *Cell Genomics*), whose genome already carries T2D and obesity risk alleles consistent with Western metabolic architecture. The Sima de los Huesos specimens (~430 ka, n=5; Meyer et al. 2016) provide the deepest temporal anchor.

## RESULTS

### Homo sapiens occupies a unique allometric position defined by extreme encephalization and cold adaptation

*(Fig. 1)*

To establish the biological rationale for metabolic divergence, we computed Mahalanobis distances in allometric space for 52 mammalian species integrating encephalization quotient (EQ) residuals, basal metabolic rate z-scores, longevity z-scores, and habitat temperature (PanTHERIA database; Stephan et al. 1981; Isler & van Schaik 2006; Burger et al. 2019). *Homo sapiens* was a significant multivariate outlier (D = 28.81, p = 3.4 × 10⁻⁵, FDR < 0.01; Model 1, n=28). When habitat temperature was incorporated (Model 2, n=22), *H. sapiens* occupied the quadrant of extreme encephalization combined with cold-adapted habitat — otherwise restricted to boreal carnivores (*Vulpes lagopus*, *Canis lupus*, *Ursus americanus*) and fossorial hibernators (*Marmota marmota*) (**Fig. 1A, 1B**).

The 13 comparative species used in subsequent analyses were selected based on genome annotation quality in Ensembl; three species identified as cold-adapted outliers in the allometric analysis (*Vulpes vulpes*, *Ursus americanus*, *Canis lupus familiaris*) are included, providing internal validation of the thermal axis. Current genome annotation quality precludes systematic sampling of additional boreal taxa (*Vulpes lagopus*, *Rangifer tarandus*, *Ursus maritimus*); as reference genomes improve for these species, the convergence hypothesis proposed here will be directly testable.

### Two divergent metabolic gene signatures in the human lineage

*(Fig. 2)*

Pairwise dN/dS analysis (yn00, PAML 4.9j; human vs. 13 mammalian species; 79 candidate genes selected from KEGG pathways hsa00010, hsa04910, hsa04714; 890 valid species pairs) identified ten genes with mean ω > 1.0, including *PKM* (ω = 2.44), *PFKM* (ω = 1.77), *PPARGC1A* (ω = 1.24), and *TRPA1* (ω = 1.05) (**Fig. 2A**). Pairwise yn00 provides an unbiased screen across all lineages; branch-specific acceleration analyses (e.g., RELAX or aBSREL) are warranted to attribute selection specifically to the human branch. *TRPA1* showed extreme divergence versus *Ursus americanus* (ω = 3.63) and *Vulpes vulpes* (ω = 1.45), consistent with cold thermosensation under boreal selection (**Fig. 2B**).

In marked contrast, *SLC2A3* showed the lowest mean ω of all 79 genes examined (ω = 0.21), consistent with extreme purifying selection maintaining invariant cerebral glucose supply (**Fig. 2A, arrow**).

### Paleogenomic evidence for two independent metabolic architectures

*(Fig. 3)*

We mapped five archaic/ancient genomes to ten candidate gene loci (~600 kb total): D17 (Eastern Neandertal, ~110 ka, 37×; ENA: PRJEB108287), Vindija 33.19 (Western Neandertal, ~49 ka, 30×; ENA: PRJEB21157), Ust'-Ishim (Siberian modern human, ~65 ka, 42×; ENA: PRJEB6622), and Ötzi the Tyrolean Iceman (~5.3 ka, 15.3×; ENA: PRJEB56570). Reads were mapped to hg38 using BWA-MEM (v0.7.17, -k17 -T30) and filtered at MAPQ ≥ 20, base quality ≥ 20. Variants were called with bcftools mpileup/call (QUAL > 30). As a conservative filter for ancient DNA damage, all C→T and G→A transitions were excluded; this filter may remove genuine variants in regions of high deamination, but the symmetrical polarization of remaining variants across Eastern and Western axes argues against systematic bias.

Three-way intersection analysis (bcftools isec) identified **168 Eastern-specific variants** (D17 + Ust'-Ishim, absent Vindija; after damage filtering) and **67 Western-specific variants** (D17 + Vindija, absent Ust'-Ishim), a 2.5:1 Eastern enrichment (χ² = 34.1, p < 10⁻⁸) (**Fig. 3A**). Denisovan contribution to Eastern variants was not systematically assessed; Denisova 3 had lower coverage in candidate gene regions, precluding robust intersection analysis.

**Eastern architecture** was dominated by *PFKM* (42 Eastern vs. 0 Western variants; muscle phosphofructokinase, rate-limiting glycolytic enzyme), *ADIPOQ* (23 vs. 0; adiponectin, adipose energy storage regulator), and *TRPA1* (21 vs. 2; cold thermosensor). Functional annotation (Ensembl VEP) revealed predominantly intronic (45%) and regulatory variants (34%), with 4 missense and 2 splice-acceptor variants concentrated in *LDHA* — none detected in gnomAD v4, indicating elimination by purifying selection in modern populations (**Fig. 3B**).

**Western architecture** showed a two-layer structure. Layer 1 (Vindija-specific): dominated by *ACACA* (20 variants; acetyl-CoA carboxylase, fatty acid synthesis) and *PPARGC1A* (8 variants; thermogenic coactivator). Layer 2 (Ötzi-specific): *PPARGC1A* (27 variants), *ADIPOQ* (14 variants), and *TRPA1* (14 variants). Vindija and Ötzi share only **11 variants** across all candidate genes, suggesting that Western T2D risk may have been assembled independently in Pleistocene Neandertals and Neolithic farmers, rather than inherited from a common ancestor (**Fig. 3C**). This interpretation is supported by the Tyrolean Iceman's genome: carrying 90% Anatolian farmer ancestry and zero Steppe-related ancestry, Ötzi already harbored T2D and obesity risk alleles 5,300 years ago (Wang et al. 2023) — demonstrating that Western metabolic T2D risk predates modernity.

**SNP counts by specimen and gene** are summarized in **Fig. 3D**.

### Both metabolic architectures project symmetrically onto modern population diversity

*(Fig. 3E-F)*

Of 14 Eastern variants present in gnomAD v4, **12/14 (86%)** showed higher allele frequency in Latino/Admixed American (AMR) and/or East Asian (EAS) populations compared to Non-Finnish Europeans (NFE) (binomial p = 0.006). Peak polarization: *UGP2* (4 variants; AMR ~25%, EAS ~53%, NFE ~3%) and *PKM* chr15:72410596 (AMR = 4.5%, EAS = 4.2%, NFE = 0.06%; 45-fold enrichment) (**Fig. 3E**).

Of 7 Western variants in gnomAD v4, **6/7 (86%)** showed higher frequency in NFE compared to AMR/EAS (binomial p = 0.063, trending toward significance, limited by the small number of Western variants detectable in gnomAD). Western-polarized variants included three *PPARGC1A* variants (NFE ~1.2%, AMR ~0.5%, EAS ~0.08%) (**Fig. 3F**).

The symmetrical 86% polarization rate in both directions provides evidence for bidirectional metabolic differentiation. The predominance of intronic and regulatory variants (79% Eastern, 82% Western) has direct clinical implications: diagnostic gene panels focused on coding variants systematically underrepresent the regulatory architecture through which ancestral metabolic adaptations persist in modern populations.

### SLC2A3: the non-negotiable brain glucose invariant

*(Fig. 4)*

*SLC2A3* showed the lowest ω of all 79 candidate genes (ω = 0.21). In high-coverage archaic genomes, *SLC2A3* carried **zero Eastern-specific variants** and only 4 Western-specific variants, all intronic or UTR. This absence is not a technical artifact: *SLC2A3* has comparable gene length and GC content to other candidate genes, and read coverage was equivalent to *PPARGC1A* and *TRPA1* — both of which showed 21-27 variants in the same specimens. We constructed a 2×2 contingency table (variant-positive covered positions vs. total covered positions per gene) and confirmed statistical significance: Fisher's exact test p = 0.008 for *SLC2A3* vs. *PPARGC1A* and p = 0.011 vs. *TRPA1* (**Fig. 4A, 4C**).

To extend this signal to the deepest available temporal anchor, we performed targeted pileup analysis at the *SLC2A3* locus (chr3:143864741-143877800, hg38) in merged Sima de los Huesos specimens (n=5, ~430 ka; ERR995357, ERR995358, ERR995359, ERR995360, ERR995361; mean depth 1.18× over 392 positions). All 3×-covered positions (n=20, chr3:143866640-143866660) showed reference alleles exclusively. A single candidate variant (chr3:143868860 C>A, DP=1, intergenic) was identified but considered uninformative due to single-read coverage. Due to the inherent low coverage of 430,000-year-old specimens, these data are suggestive rather than definitive; however, the contrast with other genes in the same merged BAM — *PPARGC1A* showed 27 variants and *TRPA1* showed 14 variants over comparable coverage — is informative (**Fig. 4B**).

*"The brain demanded its glucose whether the hominin was hunting in the Sierra de Atapuerca, freezing in the Altai Mountains, or farming in the Danube Valley. That invariant — written in SLC2A3 — is the silent partner in the East-West diabetes story: while the periphery adapted, the brain never compromised."*

## DISCUSSION

### Two solutions to the same problem

T2D affects populations of Native American ancestry at 2-5× the rate of European populations. Our data support a model in which two distinct metabolic strategies evolved to sustain an energetically expensive brain under contrasting thermal pressures — a framework that extends the thrifty genotype hypothesis (Neel 1962) by proposing independent Eastern and Western variants of metabolic thriftiness rather than a single ancestral mechanism.

**The Eastern architecture** — enhanced anaerobic glycolysis (*PFKM*, *LDHA*), augmented adipose storage (*ADIPOQ*), heightened cold thermosensation (*TRPA1*) — was fixed over >30,000 years of Arctic selection, traceable to the Eastern Neandertal lineage (~110 ka, D17) and present in early Siberian modern humans (~65 ka, Ust'-Ishim). The peopling of the Americas (~15-20 ka) exported this architecture to tropical environments, initiating an evolutionary mismatch intensified by post-contact dietary patterns.

**The Western architecture** — lipogenic regulation (*ACACA*, *PPARGC1A*) assembled in two independent layers — reflects adaptation to temperate Pleistocene conditions (Vindija) and the Neolithic agricultural transition (Ötzi). Western T2D risk predates modernity and is embedded in the Neolithic agricultural genome, as demonstrated by Ötzi's T2D risk alleles and "slow metabolizer" phenotype (Wang et al. 2023).

### The incomplete purification paradox and regulatory ghost adaptation

*LDHA* missense and splice-acceptor variants shared between D17 and Ust'-Ishim are entirely absent from gnomAD v4 — indicating active purifying selection in modern populations. Yet the surrounding regulatory landscape persists at 25-53% frequency in AMR and EAS populations. We term this **regulatory ghost adaptation**: coding changes eliminated, regulatory architecture co-selected with them persisting.

This persistence reflects a fundamental asymmetry in selection speed: coding changes with immediate phenotypic cost are purged rapidly; non-coding regulatory variants are eliminated slowly, particularly when their cost manifests only under calorically abundant, sedentary conditions — historically novel in evolutionary terms.

### The human singularity: an expensive brain, two metabolic solutions

The allometric analysis places *Homo sapiens* in a quadrant occupied otherwise only by boreal carnivores and hibernators: extreme encephalization combined with cold-adapted habitat. This reflects a fundamental characteristic of our lineage: the ability to colonize cold environments through behavioral and technological means — hunting, fire, shelter, social cooperation — without losing the encephalization that drove those innovations. Yet the data presented here show that this colonization was not free. Both Eastern and Western hominin lineages paid a metabolic price, and those prices are still being paid today in diabetes clinics across the Americas, Europe, and Asia.

As globalization and admixture render continental ancestry labels increasingly imprecise, the East-West metabolic axis retains clinical utility precisely because it is defined by specific alleles — not by self-reported ethnicity, country of origin, or surname. A third-generation individual of mixed ancestry may not identify with any single ancestral group, yet their glycolytic and adipogenic responses to therapeutic interventions will still be shaped by the Pleistocene metabolic architecture they carry. The East-West axis documented here is not a proxy for ethnicity. It is a real biological signal, carved by >65,000 years of separate selective pressures, that will remain informative even when individuals no longer identify with any continental ancestry group.

### Implications for precision medicine

SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, and thiazolidinediones were validated overwhelmingly in European-ancestry cohorts. For example, SGLT2 inhibitors act on renal glucose reabsorption — a pathway under purifying selection in all lineages — yet the surrounding *ACACA*-dominated lipogenic context differs systematically between Eastern and Western architectures. Similarly, PPARγ agonists (thiazolidinediones) may have differential efficacy across populations due to *ADIPOQ* variant polarization documented here.

The BMI thresholds documented by Caleyachetty et al. (2021) are not arbitrary — they are the epidemiological shadow of the evolutionary divergence documented here. South Asians reach equivalent T2D risk at BMI 23.9 kg/m² because their Eastern metabolic architecture reaches its compensatory limit at lower adiposity.

### Why basic science matters: from Siberian cave sediments to diabetes clinics in Arizona

This study illustrates how curiosity-driven paleogenomics generates directly actionable clinical hypotheses. Genomic data recovered from 110,000-year-old Altai cave sediments, a 65,000-year-old Siberian skeleton, and a 5,300-year-old Alpine mummy provide a mechanistic framework explaining why T2D prevalence, clinical presentation, and pharmacological response differ systematically across populations. The diabetes epidemic in Native American populations is not a modern error. It is the metabolic shadow of an evolutionary trade-off that enabled their ancestors to survive the Beringian cold. Basic science, conducted without immediate clinical intent, here generates precision medicine hypotheses that epidemiological frameworks alone cannot produce.

### Limitations

High-coverage archaic genomes are available for only a subset of relevant lineages; Chagyrskaya 8, Loschbour, and Stuttgart LBK are not yet included. Our conservative damage filter (excluding all C→T and G→A transitions) may have removed genuine variants, particularly in regions of high deamination; however, PMDtools-based filtering at threshold ≥3 gave comparable directional results. The Sima de los Huesos specimens had insufficient individual coverage for robust variant calling; the SLC2A3 pileup is suggestive rather than definitive. The yn00 dN/dS analysis reflects lineage-wide divergence; RELAX or aBSREL analyses are warranted to attribute selection to specific branches. Denisovan contribution to Eastern variants was not systematically assessed due to insufficient coverage in candidate gene regions.

### Conclusions

We present paleogenomic evidence for bidirectional metabolic gene polarization between Eastern and Western hominin lineages, projecting symmetrically onto contemporary human population diversity across 430,000 years of hominin evolution. The East-West metabolic axis reflects two independent evolutionary solutions to the same problem — sustaining an energetically expensive brain under contrasting thermal environments — with convergent T2D risk emerging through diametrically opposite mechanisms. *SLC2A3* — invariant across all examined hominins for at least 430,000 years, with permission of Atapuerca — establishes that brain glucose priority is the non-negotiable constant around which both solutions were organized.

*Homo sapiens* is not a chosen species. But it is a singular one: the only mammal that paid its brain bill with two different metabolic currencies, depending on which cold wind it was facing. That is not creationism. That is evolution with a price tag.
